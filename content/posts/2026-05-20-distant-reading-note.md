When I teach distant reading, I do not start with the triumphant version of the
story. I start with the argument.

Franco Moretti's *distant reading* is a genuinely useful provocation: stop reading
individual books closely, step back, and look at literature as a system — thousands
of titles, genres rising and falling, patterns no single reader could see. But the
most useful classroom move is to put that provocation immediately next to its
critics.

- **Johanna Drucker** reminds us that "data" in the humanities is never simply
  *given*; it is always *capta* — taken, constructed, interpreted.
- **Nan Z. Da** asks the hard, unglamorous question of whether the statistics
  actually hold up.
- **Katherine Bode** rebuilds the foundation: what is the *scholarly edition* of a
  dataset, and who curated it?

The point for students is not to choose a side. It is to learn that a method and a
critique of that method are the same intellectual object, taught together.
